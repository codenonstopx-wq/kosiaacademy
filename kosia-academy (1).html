Color Science

Understanding the color wheel —
the foundation of every formula

Complementary Relationships

Opposites cancel — that's the science of neutralization

Colors directly across the wheel from one another are complements. When mixed, they neutralize each other — this is why blue-violet toner kills yellow brassiness, and green ash cancels red. Every correction is built on this law.

RedNeutralize with Green / Blue-Green
OrangeNeutralize with Blue Ash
Yellow-OrangeNeutralize with Blue-Violet
YellowNeutralize with Violet / Purple

Warm vs. Cool Tones in Formulation

The warm half of the wheel (Reds, Oranges, Yellows) is what hair reveals as you lift. The cool half (Blues, Greens, Violets) is your neutralizing toolkit. A great colorist reads the wheel instinctively and chooses accordingly — before opening any tube.

Primary, Secondary & Tertiary

The three primaries — Red, Yellow, Blue — cannot be mixed. Every other color is born from them. Tertiaries (Red-Orange, Yellow-Orange, Yellow-Green, Blue-Green, Blue-Violet, Red-Violet) are the exact tones you encounter as underlying pigment during lift.

Placement Theory

Read the face first.
Then choose your technique.

Color placement is a deliberate act of light and shadow — designed to enhance, balance, and sculpt. Before any technique decision, a skilled colorist maps the face and asks: where do I want the eye to travel? What do I want to soften? What should I illuminate?

These principles apply to every face shape and every technique — from balayage to foil to money piece placement.

Oval
The balanced standard

The ideal proportional shape — goal is to maintain harmony, not alter it. Any technique works; client preference guides the decision.

Face-framing temple to chin, following growth pattern
Balayage flowing naturally with hair movement
Avoid over-brightening at crown — preserve balance
Any technique works; client preference guides choice
Round
Create length & definition

Full cheeks and rounded jaw. Placement should create the illusion of length. Vertical placement draws the eye up and down, not across.

Highlights at crown and center panel — not sides
Keep sides darker to visually recede width
Avoid horizontal banding at cheek level
Shadow root deepens the crown-to-tip length illusion
Square
Soften the angles

Strong jaw and wide forehead. Placement aims to soften angular corners through diagonal light flow and away-from-jaw brightness.

Balayage sweeping inward, away from jaw corners
Light highlights at temples — avoid jaw-level brightness
Diagonal placement creates softness and movement
Face-frame subtle — no hard lines at perimeter
Heart
Balance the forehead

Widest at the forehead, tapering to narrow chin. Minimize forehead width while adding brightness and fullness at mid-lengths and ends.

Minimize highlights at forehead hairline center
Add brightness at mid-lengths and ends
Keep temples mid-tone — don't brighten the widest area
Lighter ends draw eye down to chin, creating balance
Long / Oblong
Add visual width

Narrow and extended. Placement should create horizontal visual interest — the illusion of width rather than length.

Highlights through sides from temple to cheekbone
Avoid crown-heavy placement — adds perceived length
Horizontal banding at cheek level works here
Face-framing that widens apparent proportion
Diamond
Widen forehead & chin

Narrow at forehead and chin, widest at cheekbones. Add visual mass where face is narrowest — top and bottom.

Bright face-frame highlights at temples and hairline
Lighter ends draw attention to chin, creating width
Keep cheekbone area darker — don't widen widest point
Crown brightness widens apparent forehead
01
Light Advances

Lighter tones draw the eye forward and add visual weight. Where you place brightness, you create prominence — the eye travels there first.

02
Dark Recedes

Depth and shadow push areas back visually, creating the illusion of narrowing or slimming. Strategic darkness is as important as brightness.

03
Contrast Directs

The direction of contrast guides the eye. Vertical placement elongates; horizontal widens. Choose the direction that serves the face.

04
Read the Face First

No technique decision before mapping facial structure. The face is the canvas — every formula and placement is in service of it.

Core Principle

All natural hair contains underlying pigment revealed during lift. Knowing which UP appears at each level lets you predict outcomes before you formulate.

Level 5 → UP: Orange-Red
Neutralizer: Blue / Blue-Green
Level 7 → UP: Yellow-Orange
Neutralizer: Blue-Violet (diluted)
Level 9 → UP: Pale Yellow
Neutralizer: Pearl / Ice Violet
Why It Matters

Skipping this understanding leads to brassy results. Knowing your UP turns correction into prediction.

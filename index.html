Complementary Relationships

Opposites cancel — that's the science of neutralization

Colors directly across the wheel are complements. When mixed, they neutralize each other — why blue-violet toner kills yellow brassiness, and green ash cancels red. Every correction formula is built on this single law.

RedNeutralize with Green / Blue-Green
OrangeNeutralize with Blue Ash
Yellow-OrangeNeutralize with Blue-Violet
YellowNeutralize with Violet/Purple

Warm vs. Cool in Formulation

The warm half (Reds, Oranges, Yellows) is what hair reveals as you lift. The cool half (Blues, Greens, Violets) is your neutralizing toolkit. A great colorist reads the wheel instinctively before opening any tube.

Primary, Secondary & Tertiary

The three primaries — Red, Yellow, Blue — cannot be mixed. Every other color is born from them. Tertiaries are the exact tones you encounter as underlying pigment during lift — you will see them on every client.

Placement Theory

Read the face first.
Then choose your technique.

Color placement is a deliberate act of light and shadow — designed to enhance, balance, and sculpt facial features. Before any technique decision, a skilled colorist maps the face: where should the eye travel? What to soften? What to illuminate?

Oval
The balanced standard

The ideal proportional shape — goal is to maintain harmony. Any technique works; client preference guides the decision.

Face-framing temple to chin, following growth pattern
Balayage flowing naturally with hair movement
Avoid over-brightening at crown — preserve balance
Round
Create length & definition

Full cheeks and rounded jaw. Placement creates the illusion of length — vertical placement draws the eye up, not across.

Highlights at crown and center panel — not sides
Keep sides darker to visually recede width
Avoid horizontal banding at cheek level
Square
Soften the angles

Strong jaw and wide forehead. Placement softens angular corners through diagonal light flow and away-from-jaw brightness.

Balayage sweeping inward, away from jaw corners
Light highlights at temples — avoid jaw-level brightness
Diagonal placement creates softness and movement
Heart
Balance the forehead

Widest at the forehead, tapering to a narrow chin. Minimize forehead width while adding brightness at mid-lengths and ends.

Minimize highlights at forehead hairline center
Add brightness at mid-lengths and ends
Lighter ends draw eye down to chin, creating balance
Long / Oblong
Add visual width

Narrow and extended. Placement creates horizontal visual interest — the illusion of width rather than length.

Highlights through sides from temple to cheekbone
Avoid crown-heavy placement — adds perceived length
Horizontal banding at cheek level is flattering here
Diamond
Widen forehead & chin

Narrow at forehead and chin, widest at cheekbones. Add visual mass where face is narrowest — top and bottom.

Bright face-frame at temples and hairline
Lighter ends draw attention to chin
Keep cheekbone area darker — don't widen the widest point
01
Light Advances

Lighter tones draw the eye forward and add visual weight. Where you place brightness, you create prominence.

02
Dark Recedes

Depth and shadow push areas back visually — creating the illusion of narrowing or slimming.

03

Vertical placement elongates; horizontal widens. Choose the direction that serves the face.

Skin & Eye Tone Theory

The right color for every client —
reading undertones before you formulate

Hair color does not exist in isolation — it lives against a client's skin tone and interacts with their eye color every single day. Understanding warm, cool, and neutral undertones, and knowing which hair colors complement or clash with them, is what separates a technically skilled colorist from a truly transformative one. Read the person, then formulate.

The Fitzpatrick scale gives us a clinical framework for skin classification. But as colorists, we layer a second system on top: undertones — the warm (golden, peachy, yellow), cool (pink, red, bluish), and neutral (balanced mix) tones that sit beneath the surface and determine how a hair color will read in the light.

Fitzpatrick I–II
Fair Skin

Very light skin that burns easily. Undertones range from cool pink/rosy to warm peachy-ivory. The client's vein color and how they react to sun are your best indicators: blue-purple veins = cool, green veins = warm.

Cool Undertone
Best: Icy blonde, platinum, champagne, ash — cool tones echo the natural skin, creating harmony. Avoid brassy warm oranges which read harsh.
Warm Undertone
Best: Golden blonde, strawberry, warm copper, honey — golden tones amplify the peachy warmth beautifully. Avoid stark ash which can make skin look washed out.
Avoid
Jet black (too stark unless intentional), heavy orange brassiness, aggressive red-violet.

Blue Eyes
Cool · Light · Striking

Blue eyes are intensified by contrasting warm hair tones — the warm-cool interplay makes both the eyes and the hair color pop. Cool hair tones create a harmonious, soft, monochromatic look.

Enhance with
Warm golden blonde, strawberry, copper, caramel — the warmth creates contrast that makes blue irises appear more vivid and intense.
Soften with
Icy blonde, platinum, silver, cool ash — harmonizes with the cool iris for a softer, editorial look.
Avoid
Brassy orange without intention — competes with the eye colour rather than framing it.
Bold option
Deep chocolate or black creates dramatic contrast — blue eyes appear to "pop" against very dark backgrounds.
Green Eyes
Warm-Cool · Rare · Versatile

Green eyes contain both warm (yellow-green) and cool (blue-green) tones depending on the individual. They are most intensified by red and auburn tones — complementary colors on the wheel directly opposite green.

Enhance with
Auburn, warm red, copper, chestnut — red and red-brown are the complements to green on the colour wheel. These make green eyes appear deeper and more vivid.
Warm option
Golden blonde and honey amplify the yellow-green tones in the iris for a warm, radiant effect.
Cool option
Ash brown and mushroom tones harmonize with blue-green irises for a sophisticated, muted look.
Bold option
Vivid red or bright copper — green eyes against warm red hair is one of the most striking combinations in colour theory.
Hazel Eyes
Mixed · Warm · Changeable

Hazel eyes shift between green, brown, and gold depending on lighting and clothing. Their warmth makes them one of the most versatile eye tones — they respond beautifully to both warm and jewel-toned hair colours.

Enhance with
Warm auburn, amber, golden brown, copper — echoing the gold-green in hazel eyes with warm hair tones creates a cohesive, glowing result.
Jewel tones
Deep plum and rich burgundy bring out the green in hazel eyes — the contrast between warm-red hair and green iris reads beautifully.
Caramel effect
Warm caramel balayage near the face reflects warmth into the eyes, making the golden flecks in hazel irises more pronounced.
Avoid
Heavy ash tones can grey out hazel eyes and mute their warmth. Always finish with a warm toner if lightening.
Warm Brown Eyes
Warm · Rich · Universal

Warm brown eyes — amber to honey-brown — have a natural warmth and richness that pairs with almost any hair colour. The key is choosing tones that amplify the depth and luminosity of the iris rather than flattening it.

Enhance with
Warm chocolate, caramel, golden auburn, honey blonde — warm tones that echo the iris amplify the eye's natural richness and glow.
Contrast
Icy blonde and platinum create high contrast that makes warm brown eyes stand out dramatically — particularly effective on medium-dark skin tones.
Rich tones
Deep espresso, warm black, and dark chocolate with face-frame highlights create depth and make the warmth in the iris glow.
Bold option
Vivid copper or warm red — warm brown eyes against a rich copper-red base is deeply harmonious and striking.
Deep Brown Eyes
Cool-Neutral · Deep · Versatile

Deep brown eyes — nearly black — are neutral in undertone and extraordinarily versatile. The depth of the iris means almost any hair colour reads well. The goal is to choose tones that illuminate the face rather than compete with the eye.

Enhance with
Rich black, deep espresso, blue-black, dark chocolate — the iris depth is maximised when hair tones are similarly rich. Contrast occurs through face-framing highlights.
Contrast
Bold platinum or ash blonde — the high contrast between very light hair and very dark eyes is editorial and dramatic. Requires carefully chosen skin tone pairing.
Warm option
Warm chestnut and copper add warmth without competing — the deep iris holds its presence against warm tones.
Vivid option
Deep plum, navy, midnight — dark fantasy tones read beautifully because the iris depth grounds them.
Grey / Steel Eyes
Cool · Rare · Editorial

Grey and steel-blue eyes are cool and silvery — they belong firmly in the cool family and are most intensified by either harmonising cool tones or bold warm contrast. They have a unique chameleon quality that shifts in different lighting.

Harmonise
Silver, platinum, icy ash, moonstone blonde — harmonising with the cool iris creates an ethereal, monochromatic editorial look that is striking and modern.
Contrast
Warm auburn or copper — warm-cool contrast makes grey eyes appear more blue or blue-green, intensifying their colour.
Avoid
Heavy orange-brassy tones — they compete with the cool iris in an unflattering way. Warm tones must be controlled and intentional.
Bold option
Graphite or dark ash — very dark cool hair against grey eyes is an editorial statement that works particularly well with cool skin undertones.
Warm Undertones
Golden · Peachy · Yellow

Warm undertones have a golden, peachy, or yellow cast beneath the surface. Indicators: veins appear green, gold jewellery is flattering, skin tans easily. Hair colours in the warm family — golden blonde, copper, auburn, caramel — create harmony. Cool tones (ash, platinum) can look washed out or grey without a warm base toner.

01
The Vein Test

Look at the inside of the wrist in natural light. Blue or purple veins = cool undertone. Green veins = warm undertone. Blue-green = neutral. This is the fastest and most reliable reading you can do in a consultation.

02
The Jewellery Test

Does gold or silver jewellery look more flattering on the client? Gold = warm. Silver = cool. Both equally = neutral. Ask the client which they gravitate toward — they have usually already worked it out.

03
Sun Reaction

How does the skin respond to sun? Burns easily, rarely tans = cool (Fitzpatrick I–II). Tans easily, rarely burns = warm. Both = neutral. This also gives you context for how reactive the scalp and skin may be during chemical services.

04
Apply at Consultation

Run this three-point check in every new client consultation before discussing colour. Undertone knowledge changes your formulation, your toner choice, and your placement recommendation — three major decisions resolved in two minutes.

The Owner Track — Why It Exists

You didn't go to school
to learn how to run a business.
Neither did anyone else.

"The skills that built your clientele are not the same skills that will build your company. That gap is what the Owner Track is designed to close."

Cosmetology school taught you how to colour, cut, and create. It did not teach you how to read a profit and loss statement, write a job description, build a team culture, or price a service based on actual costs. Those gaps are not a personal failure — they are a gap in the education system. Most beauty professionals discover them the hard way: through a bad hire, a cash flow crisis, a team that walks out, or a full book that somehow still doesn't feel profitable.

The Philosophy Behind the Curriculum

When you understand your why,
colour becomes simple.

This is the belief at the centre of everything KOSIA teaches. Not that colour is easy — but that it becomes predictable, logical, and creative once the underlying principles are clear. Here is what we mean.

01
The Problem

Most colour education teaches steps, not principles

You are taught: use this toner, apply this developer, follow this timing. You are rarely taught: here is why this toner works, here is what the developer is doing to the hair structure, here is why timing matters based on porosity. Steps produce one result. Principles produce infinite results.

02
The Why

Underlying pigment is the entire secret

When a client's hair goes brassy, it is not a mystery — it is underlying pigment that was not neutralised. When a correction goes wrong, it is almost always because the formulator did not account for the warmth already in the hair. Understanding UP is not advanced knowledge. It is the foundation. We teach it first, and everything else clicks into place around it.

03
The Formula

The colour wheel is not art — it is science

Every toner decision, every corrective choice, every formulation you make is an application of the colour wheel. Warm cancels cool. Cool cancels warm. Complements neutralise each other. This is chemistry, not guesswork. When a colourist truly internalises the wheel — not as a chart but as a way of thinking — they stop second-guessing and start predicting.

04
The Result

Confidence is a byproduct of understanding

The stylists who feel confident at the colour bowl are not the ones who have done it the most times. They are the ones who understand what they are doing and why. Repetition without understanding builds habits. Understanding builds mastery. KOSIA is built to take you from one to the other — as fast as possible, as clearly as possible.

Formulate with Intention
Apply developer, timing, and ratio based on porosity, target result, and condition. Every variable is a decision, not a guess.

The Color Wheel & Tonal Relationships
Color Theory · Module 1 of 8 · 38 min
Core Principle

The color wheel is the single most important tool a colorist can internalize.

The three primaries cannot be created by mixing. Every shade is born from them.

Why Complements Matter

Complementary colors — directly opposite on the wheel — cancel each other out. This is why blue-violet toner neutralizes yellow brassiness and green ash corrects red underlying pigment. You are not guessing — you are applying the wheel.
